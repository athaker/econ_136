Course Outline for Spring 2020

Welcome to my course on financial derivatives and modeling. This is the second course of two finance courses offered to students at Harvey Mudd College. This course is designed to be an extension of Economics 104, my introductory finance course, which is a prerequisite for this course.

To get a better idea of the topics that we are going to cover, look at the Course Calendar. The topics to be covered include a review of measures of risk and volatility, modeling of put and call options, futures, and a lot of advanced theory. We give special emphasis to understanding and using value and pricing models and volatility models. You will be exposed to a lot of advanced trading theory.

You also experiment with some data gathering and algorithmic program trading models. We write our own models in Python and there is a substantial amount of that kind of modeling.

Course Objectives

Mostly we want to explore the exotic and fascinating world of financial derivatives. We will begin by simply understanding how they work and for what purpose they are used. Then we probe a little more deeply to develop rational explanations of their pricing and valuation, then discuss arbitrage, hedging and trading possibilities. By the end of the class, students should feel comfortable about much of this complex financial environment.

Some of you may choose to work in finance for quant funds or trading houses as investment analysts or quant traders. Every year we seem to place more students into this industry (why? because they love Mudd quants!!). This course is carefully tailored to expose you to some of the fundamental modeling and analysis common to this field. As a one-semester course we can only emphasize certain topics while ignoring others but at least you can get a taste of what is involved.

Prerequisites

This course requires that you have taken Economics 104 - Financial Economics or the equivalent at another college. Generally, a student in this class must be very familiar with the structure and operations of equity and bond markets because this course covers the market for derivatives that are derived from those fundamental markets. There is no review of those markets in this class. It is assumed that you know this material already.

We are also going to do a lot of financial modeling using the Python programming language. You must know how to use Python and also know or be willing to learn Numpy and Pandas. Some programming will be done in the classroom in a cooperative team environment which may make this a little easier.

Any student enrolled in this class should have a substantial math background. Harvey Mudd students should have no problems but if you are from another campus and have a limited math background you might want to look at some of the later lectures from last year (posted on the bottom of the E136 Lecture Slides page) to see if you can understand the material. Generally, you should understand natural logs and how they work, be able to use multi-variable calculus, be able to use or be prepared to learn Fourier Transforms and be prepared to use multiple applications of the kind of statistics that are used in Econometrics.

The Course Calendar

Either now or when you complete this course outline you should consult the Course Calendar for this class. Unlike my practice in other classes that I teach, there are no links to reading or other assignments on the Course Calendar. Instead, all links to lectures, videos, reading, homework and other assignments are found on the Lecture Slides page. Instead, the Course Calendar lays out the all-important online quiz dates and exam dates and other critical deadlines while showing how the course is divided into eight modules. Within those modules various topics are listed in the order in which they will likely be presented. As that document points out, though, as the class proceeds some of these topics might be blended, rearranged, supplemented, or even abandoned. Some of the modules are taught simultaneously. This is not a linear course!

Most of the models presented in this class are prepared before the class was offered. However, these are large, complex models, always in some stage of development, so we spend a fair amount of time together, teacher and students, continuing to improve and enhance the models.

I also add change material based in part upon the continuous dialog that I have with my students. You often come up with other interests that we pursue and you sometimes come up with better ways of presenting this complex material. And every now and then students will find a mistake in the material (especially the math) so we correct it and press on.

Reading Material and Homework Assignments

There is no textbook formally assigned to this course. There used to be, but the textbook used was intended for graduate-level instruction and as this course was refined over the years, the content drifted away from the text material, becoming specialized for Mudd students and applications that are interesting for that cohort. Additionally the course now has a major component dedicated to modeling with Python, which is not discussed in the old text. Therefore we now use the text but only as an optional reference source. It is up to you to decide whether you want to buy it. The textbook is quite good (although very difficult to read) - it is the educational bible for this kind of course. You might decide to share with another student and to buy an older out-of-print edition (there is no meaningful loss in content at all). A new edition of the text is over-priced ($229!). The textbook in question is Options, Futures, and Other Derivatives, by John C. Hull, 10th edition, ISBN 013447208X or any edition if you can get it cheaper. Do not buy this book before we meet for the first time.

Much of our class information comes from some very detailed PowerPoint slides that I will use in my lectures and from posted video lectures. We will also rely upon a lot of reading material that is available free from the internet. When the time comes we will simply find it ( I provide the URLs), download it, print it (maybe), and read it, all for free! The internet has proven to be an increasingly reliable source of good material for courses like this. All slides for my lectures are posted before the lecture on the Lecture Slides web page.

We also have some interactive course assignments which are typically Python programs. Some of these we will do together in class, and some you will do after class hours. These assignments also will be made available on the Lecture Slides web page. Postings to this page will be made as the class proceeds. I will tell you during lectures when it is time to do one of these assignments. These homework assignments are typically not corrected and are not graded, but quiz and exam questions are sometimes based upon these assignments and in some cases I may require you to bring the completed assignment to the examination to extract material for exam questions.

This class now requires that you and your team do your modeling in Python. You must have access (typically through your laptop) to Python 3 (we do not allow Python 2 to be used in this class). Your teacher knows that MatLab and R are extremely powerful packages that can be used for finance, and MatLab documentation is better than Python documentation, but we will all be working with Python 3. This is not an option.

Whereas I do not care which IDE you use with Python, I strongly recommend that you download the Anaconda distribution of Python 3, because it has all of the libraries that we will want to use. This will be discussed in an early lecture.

Class Attendance Requirements

Because we are trading collectively and working together on homework projects and algos, in this class attendance is mandatory. If you have had me for other large lecture courses you know that I don't have such a requirement there but this class is different. You are required to attend every session unless you have a reasonable excuse. And if you have a reasonable excuse (which I certainly allow) you must email me prior to the class to inform me why you will be absent. I am not likely to answer the email.

Do not take this class unless you understand this attendance rule and agree to abide by it. I will grade you down if you make a habit of missing classes regardless of how you perform on exams.

You are also required to bring a laptop to class every time we meet.

Team Assignments

Early in the semester I am going to ask the class to divide into teams of five people each (give or take one). Each team will be given some work assignments, mostly for background research. The class will also be asked to do some supplementary coding in the classroom on certain days and these assignments are undertaken by teams rather than individuals. The role played by teams will be discussed in early lectures.

Examinations and Grades

Your grade for this course will be based upon your accumulated scores from two online quizzes, a midterm exam (either both online and essay or online only), a final in-class exam, and a final online exam, plus a small team score based upon class projects. Also, as stated above, your score can be adjusted down due to lack of attendance.

The online quizzes will be taken in the last 20 minutes of class time on the dates scheduled in the Course Calendar.

Grade weighting:

  • Twenty-minute online quizzes: 15% each
  • Mid-term exam (including online portion): 30%
  • Final exam (including online portion): 30%
  • Team participation and class attendance: 10%

The final 10% of your grade will be determined by your contribution to class and team projects, including class attendance. With reasonable good-faith effort, you should be able to get full credit for this 10% of your grade.

Your final grade will be based upon a final distribution for the class after the scores above have been posted. The cutoff points for different letter grades are not determined until I have evaluated that final distribution. If you are concerned about your grade prior to the final, do not ask me what score you will have to earn to meet your grade target. I will not know because I will not have seen the final distribution. I don’t (really can’t) give extra credit assignments to compensate for low exam scores. There is no extra credit and there are no makeups.

You have the right to appeal a grade on an exam so long as the following procedure is used:

To appeal a grade on an essay exam question, you must submit to me the basis of your complaint (explaining why you should have been given a higher score) in writing on an 8 1/2" by 11" sheet of paper within a week of receiving back your graded exam. I will be willing to talk to you at length about your exam, but not about changing the score on your exam, unless you have first followed the procedure above. To this rule I make one exception: if I have improperly posted your grade because I made a mistake adding the score, you need only tell me.

Policy on Pass/No Pass: I automatically allow any student who wishes to take the course on a P/NP basis to do so if the student is eligible. HOWEVER, to receive a passing grade the student must have accumulated enough points to receive the equivalent C- or better! Prior to the final, do not ask me how many points you must earn on the final to pass. I will not tell you because I will not know. Your final grade will depend upon the final distribution after the final has been taken. Also, if you are P/NP status, to earn a passing grade for this class, regardless of your prior scores, you must pass the final exam in-class exam and the final take-home exam if one is assigned. If you receive a score on the final exam alone that I deem to be a failing grade, and you are registered as a P/NP student, I will assign an NP for the course. This policy does not necessarily apply to students taking the course for a grade (because you are merely going to get a very low grade, possibly an F).

About the somewhat unstructured nature of this course

This course covers some very complex material at a very advanced level. This course is taught at a level used in the second year of a Ph.D. program in finance (based upon the book used in this course). This material can be very challenging for both the student and the teacher. This stuff is difficult to teach. Also this course is extremely lecture-dependent. The reference reading material is difficult to understand without explanation. Further, although the scheduling of material in this course appears to be linear - we actually work simultaneously on different tasks, as is explained in the Course Calendar. Also, the pace of progress varies because I will not move on from one topic to the next until I am sure the class understands it (it builds in the major sections - if you don't understand step 1 you won't understand step 2). I experiment with how I teach these topics. I constantly discard or change lectures. Some thrusts fail. In some areas we have to backtrack.

Video and Audio

You are not allowed to use video or audio recording equipment in my classroom without my express written permission (permission by email constitutes written permission). If you have some reason to do it, you might ask and I might allow it. There are two reasons for this: (1) all of my material including oral presentations is my copyrighted material and I have the right to control it, and more important, (2) unauthorized recordings can have a stifling affect upon free and open speech. What goes on in the classroom is between you and me, not you and me and everyone who watches YouTube.

The YouTube live recordings that I use in some other courses are not used in this course. On the other hand, a lot of the lecture material is presented as video. You are as responsible for video content as you are in-class lectures.
