Welcome to Economics 53, Introduction to Macroeconomics. This course is beginning at a time when economic growth is steady, trade skirmishes with international trading partners, especially China, continue to threaten growth but are stabilizing, and our nation's central banking authority, the Federal Reserve System, is still searching for an optimal policy while a major election looms on the horizon. Meanwhile the stock market had an explosive 12 months while Bitcoin and the crypto-currencies try to rebound from a weak year. From an economics perspective, this is going to be an interesting year.

So what lies ahead?

This course is inherently topical. We will read talk about these subjects and many others that are related. We will study the economic topics that you often read about in the newspaper: inflation, deflation, interest rate behavior, employment and unemployment, international trade, exchange rates, the financial markets, business cycles, national production, taxes, the federal budget, budget deficits, Social Security, and so forth. In a few words, we will investigate the American economy at an introductory level.

You are not required to purchase a textbook for this course. This course uses online reading material, audio podcasts, and videos only. We will use five chapters from an internet-based macroeconomics modeling book written by me. These chapters are placed on the web site for this class in PDF format. This will enable you to display and print them as you need them. Many of the reading assignments for this course are drawn from U.S. government and other web sites. The reading assignments and the sources are found on the Course Calendar page and an organized copy of the sources are also found on the E53 Texts page.

PowerPoint versions of all lectures in this class are provided in advance (typically on the day or the day before they are given) on the Lecture Slides and Course Content page. Other support material (not much) and archival lectures from last year, along with video recordings of current lectures posted after they are given, are also posted on this site.

Course Objectives

In this class I offer a general overview of the U.S. economy in the hope that you will retain some of it after the course has ended. We explore such topics as economic growth, business cycles, the structure of the economy, employment and unemployment, inflation, and government monetary and fiscal policy. To some extent I hope to show you how economists identify and think about economic problems. There is some exposure to theory and modeling in this class so you can see how we organize general economic concepts into analytic models.

Reading Strategy

It really helps if you read the assigned material (or listen to it or watch it) before lectures on the subject (it helps me, actually). The reading sets the stage and in some cases eliminates my need to talk about certain things. Instead of a single expensive textbook, we are drawing our reading material from a variety of sources, all of which are free. You must try to discipline yourself to check the Course Calendar for the weekly or sectional reading assignments, to find them on the internet, to print them and/or to read them.

Revised Grade Policy

Your grade will be based upon scores from the first traditional exam and four online quizzes that are given after April 6th.

The results of the first exam will be reduced from 200 possible points to 50 possible points to give it the same weight as the other exams. (Example: if you had a score of 176 on the first exam, that will be reduced to a 44 of 50).

The quizzes will not have equal weight (although they will be close in weight).

Prof Evans will retain your four best scores from the five exams and base your grade upon a percentage distribution of that. As promised elsewhere, grading will be generous compared to normal semesters.

Policy on Pass/No Pass: You are allowed to take this course P/NC by merely asking. There are no restrictions.

Class Attendance and Email Notifications

I don't require students to attend my lectures. However, please understand that this is a lecture-based course. I pull a lot of material together in the lectures. From years past I have noticed that there is some correlation between class attendance and grades.

Having access to the lectures on YouTube makes it easier to miss live lectures and many students in the past have stated that they appreciate the freedom provided by this option. Beware, however, that in past semesters some students have abused this privilege and have seen their grades consequently suffer. The failure rate for both Economics 53 and Economics 104, where I also allow this privilege, has risen in recent years because students who fall behind in the lectures and plan to catch up never get around to it.

Also please understand that I ask some questions on exams about material that is covered only in the lecture and not in the written material nor in the slides (which are general summary documents anyway).

Because many students do miss class, I now impose certain rules. First, if you are absent for any reason it is your responsibility to obtain the class material that you missed, but given that all of it is online, including lecture slides, that shouldn't be a problem. Finally, although I sometimes allow students to reschedule exams for legitimate reasons (typically job interviews, clinic excursions, or illness), I cannot reschedule exams for students who are overloaded with work. Please realize that if you anticipate a heavy schedule around exam time, try to schedule your work so that there is less of a crush.

Because class attendance is optional I do require students to check all email that is sent by me regarding this class. Email messages are now considered part of class content and as a student in this class you are required to read your email within a reasonable period of time. Those emails often include notifications and reminders of due dates or material that might be on an exam and you are accountable for that material.

Course Conflicts Between This Course and Another Course

Because this course offers all lectures online, students sometimes ask me if I allow course conflicts with other courses scheduled at the same time, as I do in Economics 104. Alas, in this class course conflicts are not allowed. Conflicts are allowed in Economics 104 because there is no replacement course for Economics 104. But there are many Macroeconomics courses offered at the 5Cs. Course conflicts involve a lot of extra work for me at exam time, so for the time-being I follow a no-conflict policy,

Video and Audio

You are not allowed to use video or audio recording equipment in my classroom without my express written permission (permission by email constitutes written permission). If you have some reason to do it, you might ask and I might allow it. There are two reasons for this prohibition: (1) all of my material including oral presentations is copyrighted material that belongs to me and I have the right to control it, and more important, (2) unauthorized recordings can have a stifling affect upon free and open speech. What goes on in the classroom is between you and me, not you and me and everyone who watches YouTube.
